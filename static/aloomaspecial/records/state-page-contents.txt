Why #State Properties Face Distinct Moisture Challenges<h2>
The way moisture moves through a building depends on three things: the building's construction, the moisture source, and the local environmental conditions. In #State, all three factors combine in ways that make professional remediation particularly important.
Older homes across #State were built with materials that absorb water readily and release it slowly. Original plaster walls — common in pre-1950s construction — can retain significant moisture long after a water event appears to have resolved on the surface. Subfloor materials in mid-century construction are often board-sheathed rather than plywood-panel, which means they absorb and hold moisture in fundamentally different ways than sheet goods do. That moisture creates conditions for wood rot and mold colonization at the structural level, where it is hardest to detect and most expensive to address.
Modern construction in #State presents the inverse challenge. High-performance insulation, vapor-retarding membranes, and engineered wood products have made homes far more energy-efficient — but far less forgiving when moisture gets in. Engineered wood loses structural integrity significantly faster than dimensional lumber under repeated wetting cycles. Spray foam insulation that traps moisture against a structural member can support mold growth for years without producing any visible sign on the interior finish surface.
JR Restoration accounts for construction type from the first moment of assessment in #State. Before extraction equipment goes down, we walk the property, identify the building materials involved, and build a drying strategy calibrated to those materials. Mold in #State — How Local Conditions Accelerate Growth in Places You Don't Expect<h2>
Mold remediation in #State requires understanding how humidity and temperature patterns interact with building materials across different seasons. This is not a generic observation — it directly changes where mold hides and how fast it spreads.
During periods of elevated humidity, the stack effect — the natural upward movement of air through a building as warm air rises — pulls moisture-laden air from the lowest levels of a structure up through the living space. In #State homes with crawl spaces, this means ground moisture evaporates from exposed soil, rises through gaps in the subfloor, travels through the living space, and condenses against cooler surfaces in attic assemblies or exterior wall cavities. The result is mold growth that appears in locations far removed from any visible water source. The attic develops a mold problem whose origin is the crawl space two floors below.
During drier seasons, condensation forms on cold surfaces — window frames, exterior walls, uninsulated pipe runs in unconditioned spaces. These small but persistent moisture deposits accumulate over time. Eventually they support mold colonization in places that never experienced a flood and never showed a water stain.
JR Restoration's mold inspections in #State use calibrated thermo-hygrometers, surface moisture meters, and borescope cameras — fiber optic inspection tools that see inside wall cavities through a small drilled port — to locate mold wherever it is actually growing. We do not limit our inspection to what is visible on the surface.

Is water damage from a roof leak treated the same way as basement flooding? The source of the water changes the classification and sometimes the decontamination requirements. Roof leak water is generally Category 2 — gray water — though it can move toward Category 3 if it contacts biological contamination in the attic space, which is common in #State properties where animals have accessed attic areas. The restoration sequence — extract, dry, inspect for mold, remediate if needed, then reconstruct — applies in both cases.
